Research Context

The Mincer Wage Equation

A foundational model in labour economics, linking human capital to wage outcomes.

🎓

Human Capital Theory

Becker (1964) formalised the idea that individuals invest in education and training — just as firms invest in capital — to raise future earning capacity. Each additional year of schooling increases productivity and thus wages.

📈

Returns to Experience

On-the-job learning raises productivity. However, returns diminish over time as human capital depreciates. The experience-squared term captures this inverted-U: wages rise rapidly early in career, then slow and eventually decline.

⚖️

Gender Wage Gap

After controlling for education and experience, male workers in the IHDS-II sample earn approximately 42.0% more than female workers. This reflects occupational segregation and systematic wage discrimination in Indian labour markets.

🏙️

Urban–Rural Divide

Urban workers earn approximately 14.7% more than rural workers, reflecting higher labour productivity, sectoral composition, and cost-of-living differentials. Urbanisation significantly shapes India's wage structure.

Why a semi-log model?

The Mincer equation regresses ln(wage) on human capital variables. This semi-logarithmic form is theoretically appropriate when the rate of return to each additional year of schooling is approximately constant — implying wages grow exponentially with schooling. The log transformation also reduces right-skewness in the wage distribution common in cross-sectional data.

Dataset: India Human Development Survey-II (IHDS-II, 2011–12), conducted by the University of Maryland and NCAER. Working sample: 5,000 wage-employed individuals with complete data on wages, education, age, gender, and urban/rural status. Parameters estimated via OLS with HC1 robust standard errors.

F-statistic (Wald)

On the Adjusted R² of 0.2005

The model explains approximately 20.1% of variation in log wages. This is consistent with cross-sectional labour economics wage regressions in the literature — Dutta (2006) and Psacharopoulos & Patrinos (2004) report typical R² values of 0.15–0.30 in individual-level earnings data. Wages are influenced by numerous unobserved factors: occupation, industry, caste, firm size, networks, and innate ability. Labour economics models prioritise coefficient interpretation and structural relationships over prediction precision. The joint F-statistic of 214.59 (p < 0.001) confirms strong overall explanatory power.

Model Reliability

Diagnostics & Model Validity

Four CLRM assumption tests conducted on full model residuals. Where violations occur, appropriate corrections have been applied.

Jarque-Bera Test

Normality of Residuals · JB = 1,262.0
Violated
▸ Interpretation
p < 0.001 indicates significant non-normality. This is common in large cross-sectional wage datasets. With n = 5,000, the Central Limit Theorem ensures OLS estimators remain approximately normally distributed, so inference remains valid. No corrective action required at this sample size.

VIF Analysis

Multicollinearity · Edu: 1.55 · Exp: 12.25 · Exp²: 11.65
Moderate (Expected)
▸ Interpretation
Education, Male, and Urban VIFs < 2.0 — well within acceptable bounds. High VIF for Experience/Experience² (12.25/11.65) is structurally expected: Exp² is a mathematical function of Exp. This polynomial multicollinearity inflates standard errors for experience coefficients, but high t-statistics (17.44 and −13.10) confirm adequate estimation precision. Not a concern for inference.

Breusch-Pagan Test

Homoskedasticity · LM = 177.94
Violated → Corrected
▸ Interpretation
p < 0.001 strongly rejects homoskedasticity. Heteroskedasticity is common in wage regressions: earnings variance differs across education and experience levels. While this does not bias coefficients, it renders conventional standard errors inconsistent. Corrected by applying HC1 (Eicker-Huber-White) heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors throughout — ensuring valid t-statistics and F-statistic.

Breusch-Godfrey LM Test

Serial Dependence · LM = 157.95
Contextual
▸ Interpretation
p < 0.001 rejects no serial dependence. In a cross-sectional dataset, this most plausibly reflects household/village-level clustering in the IHDS-II survey design, or omitted group-level variables — not true time-series autocorrelation (IHDS-II has no temporal ordering). This reinforces the case for cluster-robust standard errors in future work. The HC1 correction applied here provides a conservative improvement over naive OLS inference.

Limitations & Transparency

⚠ Experience Proxy
Potential experience (Age − Education − 6) may contain measurement error, especially for women with interrupted careers.
⚠ Omitted Variables
Caste, occupation, industry, firm size are excluded. May introduce omitted variable bias in education/experience coefficients.
⚠ No Causal Identification
Cross-sectional data cannot establish causality. Education coefficients capture conditional correlations, not causal returns. IV methods required for causal inference.
⚠ Sample Restriction
Sample restricted to wage employees. Self-employed workers (a large share of India's labour force) are excluded. Results may not generalise to all workers.

Mathematical Specification

The Econometric Model

The full Mincer wage equation estimated on IHDS-II data (Model 4 — full specification).

ln(Wageᵢ) = 1.9069 + 0.0556 · Educationᵢ + 0.0359 · Experienceᵢ − 0.0004 · Experienceᵢ² + 0.3510 · Maleᵢ + 0.1368 · Urbanᵢ + uᵢ

Wage-Maximising Experience

∂ln(W)/∂Exp = β₂ + 2β₃·Exp = 0
Exp* = −β₂ / (2β₃)
      = −0.0359 / (2 × −0.0004)
      ≈ 44.9 years

Beyond 44.9 years of experience, the negative experience-squared term dominates and wages decline — consistent with human capital depreciation in the Mincer framework.

Exact Dummy Variable Premiums

% Difference = (e^β − 1) × 100

Male premium:
(e^0.3510 − 1) × 100 ≈ 42.0%

Urban premium:
(e^0.1368 − 1) × 100 ≈ 14.7%

Log-linear interpretation

In the semi-log specification, the coefficient on a continuous regressor gives the approximate proportional change in wages for a one-unit increase. For example, β₁ = 0.0556 implies that one additional year of schooling is associated with approximately 5.56% higher hourly wages, holding all else constant. For binary dummy variables, the exact percentage differential requires the exponential transformation: (e^β − 1) × 100%.

Econometric Wage Dynamics Explorer

"An Econometric Analysis of the Mincer Wage Equation: The Association of Education and Experience with Wages in India"

Nikhil J Palakeel & Dhruv Aryan · BA Programme · St Stephen's College · Basic Econometrics ECON24

This tool provides econometric model-based wage estimates derived from IHDS-II (2011–12) survey data and inflation-adjusted approximations for interpretive purposes. The model is designed primarily to explore wage determinants and relative economic relationships rather than provide precise salary forecasts. Actual wages depend on numerous additional factors including occupation, industry, geography, skills, labour market conditions, macroeconomic change, and unobserved individual characteristics. This tool is intended for educational and analytical purposes only.

Dataset: IHDS-II (NCAER & University of Maryland, 2012) · OLS with HC1 Robust Standard Errors · Adj. R² = 0.2005 · n = 5,000
